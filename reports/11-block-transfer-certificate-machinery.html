The Block-Transfer Certificate: How the Reach Bound Is Actually Computed

Zhibai Jia · 8 July 2026 · updated 9 Jul: §4 now carries the measured tightness trilemma — scalar / directional / Newton–Kantorovich; 10 Jul: new §0 recaps the $\sigma_{\min}$ saga — approximation-theory origin → response height → two failed conditioning fixes; 12 Jul: certificate-map table in §4 (granularity × direction, + NK and the reader-set escape) · a short reference note — the arc from the resolvent to a certified reach, one scalar mnemonic that carries the whole shape, and the subtle three-way play of $d$, $r$, and eff_rate

What this note is. A compact, self-contained account of the machine that turns the linearized operator $M=I-J$ into a certified statement "an edit at position $j$ moves position $i$ by at most this much." Notes #9–#10 established why we need it (the scalar Faber routes are vacuous / abstain); this one records how the working certificate runs, so the derivation is in one place before drafting.

0. Why $\sigma_{\min}(I-J)$? — the saga, from approximation theory to response height

$\sigma_{\min}(I-J)$ has been in the campaign from the start, but what it means has changed twice. Worth one section before the machinery, because the whole point of this note is that $\sigma_{\min}$ is not the reach rate — and learning that took two turns.

(a) Origin — approximation theory (the decay of a banded inverse). $\sigma_{\min}$ first entered as a conditioning number. Classical results on off-diagonal decay of matrix inverses — Demko–Moss–Smith for banded matrices, Benzi–Golub for the symmetric case — bound $\big\|[M^{-1}]_{ij}\big\|\le C\,q^{|i-j|}$ with a rate $q$ read straight off the conditioning: for symmetric-positive $M$, $q=(\sqrt\kappa-1)/(\sqrt\kappa+1)$ with $\kappa=\sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}$. So a near-singular $M$ (small $\sigma_{\min}$) means large $\kappa$, slow decay, and long edit-reach. That gave the first reach envelope — the "$\sqrt\kappa$ Faber number." $\sigma_{\min}$ sat squarely in the rate.

(b) The crisis — the approximation-theory rate abstains (Notes #9–#10). The $\sqrt\kappa$ number turned out to be a heuristic: a Hermitian theorem applied to a non-Hermitian $J$. Its sharp non-normal generalization (Faber on the field of values / Crouzeix) abstains on both faces, because $0\in W(I-J)$ — the numerical range straddles the singularity even though $\rho(J)<1$ ($+1\in W(J)$; $W$ huge, $\mathrm{Re}\,W\approx3.1$, despite $\rho\approx0.74$). No field-of-values rate is certifiable, and the crude normal-equations fallback (Route A) certifies but is vacuous (~222 hops on five windows). Verdict: $\sigma_{\min}$ cannot carry the reach rate (measured, c2_bidir / c2_weighted_cert; the full autopsy is in #9–#10).

(c) The pivot — rate $\to\rho(G)$, $\sigma_{\min}\to$ height. The reach rate moved off approximation theory onto dynamical systems / Lyapunov — the block-transfer $\rho(G)$ plus the Stein adapted-norm of §§1–3. That freed $\sigma_{\min}$ from a job it could not do and revealed its true one: not a rate but an amplitude. $\|M^{-1}\|=1/\sigma_{\min}$ is the peak gain of the resolvent — how tall the edit-response $\delta z = M^{-1}\delta h$ can stand per unit edit. Near-singular = near-resonance = a tall response that rings; well-conditioned = a short, quiet one. $\sigma_{\min}$ is the reciprocal response height, $\rho(G)$ is the reach, and §5 keeps the two apart.

The one-line reframe. $\sigma_{\min}$ stopped being "the conditioning number in a decay-of-inverse bound" (approximation theory, which abstains here) and became "the height of the edit-response" (the amplitude/gain of the resolvent $M^{-1}$). It sets scale, not shape. Both it and $\sqrt{\kappa(P)}$ are "heights," but of different operators: $1/\sigma_{\min}$ is the height of the response itself; $\sqrt{\kappa(P)}$ (§1e) is the transient hump of the reach operator $G$. Do not conflate them.

(d) $\sigma_{\min}$ today — three jobs, all measured.

  • Positivity gate. $\sigma_{\min}>0 \Leftrightarrow I-J$ invertible $\Leftrightarrow$ the fixed point is locally well-posed $\Leftrightarrow$ the resolvent exists — upstream of everything. Local, not global: curr40 has $\sigma_{\min}>0$ at each branch yet is globally multistable (two solvers → two distinct fixed points, solver-checked), so $\sigma_{\min}$ certifies a branch, not uniqueness of the world.
  • Response height / a-posteriori amplifier. The deployable bound $\|z-z^{\star}\|\le\|f(z)-z\|/\sigma_{\min}$ (§4) is $\sigma_{\min}$-as-height: the residual, amplified by the gain. The pre-solve forecast $\|R\,\delta h\|\to\|\Delta z\|$ (Spearman 0.96–1.00 on both faces — the campaign's most robust relationship) is the same height read directionally; the tightness trilemma (§4) is where the scalar height over-charges.
  • The height you cannot wish away (conditioning↔recall). Two independent architectural attempts to lift $\sigma_{\min}$ on the near-singular currnp substrate both failed by collapsing recall: QK-norm (cosine attention) doubled $\sigma_{\min}$ but dropped recall 15–24 pts (capping logit magnitude starves the peaky attention long-range recall needs); RoPE — the orthogonal, norm-preserving alternative that cannot cap peaking — instead disrupts the cross-window relay outright, present or absent the learned relative bias (recall plateaus ~0.80 vs a clean 1.0). The near-singularity is intrinsic to a model that actually remembers: edit-locality is dual to forgetting, and $\sigma_{\min}$ is load-bearing, not an artifact to engineer away.

The rest of this note is the other invariant — the reach rate $\rho(G)$ and the machine that certifies it — with $\sigma_{\min}$ returning in §4 as the deployable residual amplifier and in §5 as its sibling.

1. The arc, in one pass

We study the resolvent $M^{-1}$, where $M = I - J$ and $J$ is the Jacobian of the equilibrium map at the fixed point. The block $[M^{-1}]_{ij}$ is the linear response of position $i$ to a perturbation at position $j$ — the object whose decay is edit-locality.

(a) Block structure from windowing. Attention only couples a token to its own window and its neighbours (Moore-neighbourhood / NCA-land), so after reblocking into $w$-token windows, $M$ is block-tridiagonal: diagonal blocks are within-window coupling, off-diagonals are the only cross-window transport.

(b) Split off the diagonal (precondition by $D$). Let $D=\mathrm{blockdiag}(M)$ collect the within-window blocks. Factor

$$ M = D\,(I - G), \qquad G := -\,D^{-1}(M-D) = I - D^{-1}M. $$

This is the block-Jacobi splitting. $D^{-1}$ absorbs all the local (in-window) coupling once and for all; $G$ is the leftover normalized cross-window transport operator — one application of $G$ = one hop between windows. Where does the "$I-$" come from? It is just $I - D^{-1}M$ rearranged from $D^{-1}M = I - G$: solving $Mx=b$ by the fixed-point iteration $x\leftarrow D^{-1}(b + (D-M)x)$ has iteration matrix exactly $G$.

(c) The resolvent as a hop series. From $M=D(I-G)$,

$$ M^{-1} = (I-G)^{-1}D^{-1} = \Big(\textstyle\sum_{k\ge 0} G^{k}\Big) D^{-1}. $$

This cleanly separates the two jobs: $D^{-1}$ handles the within-window amplitude (the "scale"), and $G^k$ handles reaching across $k$ windows (the "shape"). The block $[M^{-1}]_{ij}$ between windows $d$ apart is dominated by $G^{d}$ — so the entire reach question reduces to how fast $\|G^k\|$ decays.

(d) $\rho(G)$ is the truth but it lies at finite $k$. The asymptotic decay rate of $\|G^k\|$ is the spectral radius $\rho(G)$ (Gelfand). But $G$ is non-normal, so $\|G^k\|$ can grow for many steps before it decays — a transient hump. A naive $\|G^k\|\le\|G\|^k$ bound is useless ($\|G\|\approx5\text{–}22$), and $\rho(G)^k$ is not an upper bound at finite $k$. We need something that honours the true rate and covers the hump.

(e) The adapted-norm (Stein/Lyapunov) certificate. Pick a target rate $r\in(\rho(G),1)$. Solve the discrete Lyapunov / Stein equation for the symmetric positive $P$:

$$ (G/r)^{*} P\,(G/r) - P = -I \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\; P = \sum_{k\ge0}\big((G/r)^{*}\big)^{k}(G/r)^{k}. $$

$P$ is the observability Gramian of $G/r$ — it accumulates the total future energy of the scaled operator, so it is large in exactly the directions that transiently amplify, and re-weighting by it flattens the hump. In the $P$-norm $\|x\|_P=\sqrt{x^*Px}$, $G/r$ is a genuine contraction:

$$ \|(G/r)\,x\|_P \le c_P\,\|x\|_P,\qquad c_P := \|G/r\|_P < 1. $$

Iterating $k$ times and converting the $P$-norm back to Euclidean (the exchange rate is $\sqrt{\kappa(P)}$; in code $\sqrt{\lambda_{\max}(P)}$, valid since $P\succeq I$) gives the certified per-term bound

$$ \boxed{\;\|G^{k}\|_2 \le \sqrt{\kappa(P)}\;\big(\underbrace{r\,c_P}_{\texttt{eff\_rate}}\big)^{k}.\;} $$

The non-normality is now quarantined into the constant $\sqrt{\kappa(P)}$; the base eff_rate$=r\,c_P$ is a hair below $r$ and carries the decay.

(f) Back to the resolvent block. Sum the tail from hop $d$ onward and reattach $\|D^{-1}\|$:

$$ \big\|[M^{-1}]_{ij}\big\| \;\le\; \|D^{-1}\|\sum_{k\ge d}\|G^k\| \;\le\; \frac{\sqrt{\kappa(P)}\,\|D^{-1}\|}{1-\texttt{eff\_rate}}\;\texttt{eff\_rate}^{\,d}. $$

That is the whole certificate: a clean geometric envelope in the block-distance $d$, with a certified rate and a finite constant, no normality assumption anywhere.

(g) Where eff_rate comes from, and why it isn't exactly $r$

Write $\tilde G = G/r$. The Stein equation is the statement $\tilde G^{*}P\tilde G = P - I$, so reading it in the $P$-norm gives a one-line identity:

$$ \|\tilde G x\|_P^{2} = x^{*}\tilde G^{*}P\tilde G\,x = x^{*}(P-I)x = \|x\|_P^{2} - \|x\|_2^{2}. $$

Every hop drains a full unit of Euclidean energy $\|x\|_2^2$ out of the $P$-energy. The $P$-operator-norm is the sup of that ratio over directions, and $\lambda_{\max}(P)$ enters as the worst one:

$$ c_P^{2} = \sup_{x\ne0}\frac{\|\tilde G x\|_P^{2}}{\|x\|_P^{2}} = \sup_x\frac{\|x\|_P^{2}-\|x\|_2^{2}}{\|x\|_P^{2}} = 1 - \inf_x\frac{x^{*}x}{x^{*}Px} = 1 - \frac{1}{\lambda_{\max}(P)}, $$

since $\sup_x \tfrac{x^{*}Px}{x^{*}x}=\lambda_{\max}(P)$ (Rayleigh quotient), so the infimum is $1/\lambda_{\max}(P)$, attained at $P$'s top eigenvector. Hence

$$ c_P = \|\tilde G\|_P = \sqrt{1 - \tfrac{1}{\lambda_{\max}(P)}} < 1, \qquad \texttt{eff\_rate} = r\,c_P < r. $$

eff_rate is below $r$ because the "$-I$" hands back a bit more contraction than you asked for. Why the max: the drain is a fixed unit of Euclidean energy; in the direction $P$ stretches most (eigenvalue $\lambda_{\max}(P)$) the $P$-energy is largest relative to $\|x\|_2^2$, so that unit is the smallest fraction of it — the weakest contraction. The worst-draining mode sets the rate, and that mode is exactly the peak accumulated transient energy $\lambda_{\max}(P)$. Note it appears twice: the same $\lambda_{\max}(P)$ sets this tiny rate correction and the large constant ($\sqrt{\kappa(P)}$, in code $\sqrt{\lambda_{\max}(P)}$ since $P\succeq I$) — one number governs both.

But the "$-I$" drain is not a non-normal effect — it is present for any $G$. What non-normality controls is how much of the true decay the drain can recover:

  • $G$ normal ($G=U\Lambda U^{*}$): $\tilde G^{*}$ and $\tilde G$ commute, so $(\tilde G^{*})^{k}\tilde G^{k}=(\tilde G^{*}\tilde G)^{k}$ and the Gramian collapses to a rational function of $G$, $$ P = \sum_{k\ge0}(\tilde G^{*}\tilde G)^{k} = (I - \tilde G^{*}\tilde G)^{-1}, $$ diagonal in $G$'s eigenbasis with entries $1/(1-|\lambda_i/r|^{2})$. Then $\lambda_{\max}(P)=1/\!\big(1-(\rho/r)^{2}\big)$, so $c_P=\rho/r$ and $\texttt{eff\_rate}=r\cdot(\rho/r)=\rho(G)$ exactly. The drain squeezes the injected $r$ all the way back to the true rate, $\sqrt{\kappa(P)}$ stays modest, and the adapted norm was never needed — a normal operator already has $\|G^{k}\|_2=\rho^{k}$ with no transient.
  • $G$ non-normal: the transient hump inflates $\lambda_{\max}(P)$, so $1/\lambda_{\max}(P)\approx0$, $c_P\approx1$, and $\texttt{eff\_rate}\approx r$ — the drain recovers almost none of the gap down to $\rho$, and the entire non-normality bill is paid in the large $\sqrt{\kappa(P)}$ instead. This is exactly what our runs show: bidir16 has $\rho(G)=0.332$ yet $\texttt{eff\_rate}\approx r\approx0.38$ (well above $\rho$), with the prefactor $\approx92$ carrying the cost.
The honest reading. The "$-I$" drain is what lets eff_rate dip below $r$; non-normality is what stops it from reaching $\rho$. In the normal limit $P$ degenerates into $(I-\tilde G^{*}\tilde G)^{-1}$ and the whole certificate collapses to the elementary geometric bound at rate $\rho$ with constant $1$. Our operators are far from that limit, which is precisely why the reach lives in $\rho(G)$ but the price shows up in $\sqrt{\kappa(P)}$.
The lineage, in one line. This is not approximation theory (Demko–Moss–Smith / Faber-on-the-field-of-values, which abstain here). It is dynamical-systems / RNN-Lyapunov: a discrete Lyapunov function $V(x)=\|x\|_P^2$ with $V(Gx)one lineage; causal is the degenerate corner where $G$ is strictly block-triangular, hence nilpotent, $\rho(G)=0$, and the series terminates.

(h) The Lyapunov reading: stored energy, dissipation, and why the Euclidean norm misleads

The Stein equation is the discrete-time twin of the continuous Lyapunov equation, and laying them side by side names every object in play here — and gives a physics mnemonic for what the adapted norm is actually doing.

Continuous. For a flow $\dot x = Ax$, pick a quadratic energy $V(x)=x^{*}Px$. Its rate of change along the flow is $$ \dot V = \dot x^{*}Px + x^{*}P\dot x = x^{*}(A^{*}P+PA)\,x. $$ Demanding $\dot V=-x^{*}Qx$ with $Q\succ0$ is the continuous Lyapunov equation $A^{*}P+PA=-Q$: the stored energy $V$ strictly decreases, and a second quadratic form $x^{*}Qx$ is the instantaneous dissipation rate — the power bled out of the system. (In mechanics $V$ is the total mechanical energy and $x^{*}Qx$ is the Rayleigh dissipation function.)

Discrete. For a hop map $x_{k+1}=Gx_k$ the time-derivative becomes a per-step difference, $$ \Delta V = V(Gx)-V(x) = x^{*}(G^{*}PG-P)\,x, $$ and demanding $\Delta V=-x^{*}Qx$ is exactly the Stein equation $G^{*}PG-P=-Q$. Same statement, "per unit time" replaced by "per window-hop": $V$ is a stored energy that drops by a fixed quadratic amount at every hop of the block-transfer operator.

Which is which, here.

  • $G$ — the block-transfer flow map (one window-hop; the discrete analogue of the semigroup $e^{At}$).
  • $V(x)=x^{*}Px$ — the stored (adapted) energy, and our Lyapunov function. $P$ is the observability Gramian, so $V(x)=\sum_{k\ge0}\|G^{k}x\|_2^{2}$ is literally the total future energy the perturbation $x$ will emit as it propagates — energy in the bank, not yet released.
  • $Q=I$ — the dissipation. Each hop drains exactly one unit of Euclidean energy $x^{*}Ix=\|x\|_2^{2}$. The "$-I$" on the right of the Stein solve (§1e) is not a normalization convenience; it is the dissipation term — the same "$-I$ drain" of §1g, now named — and it is what makes $V$ a strict Lyapunov function.
  • eff_rate — the certified geometric rate at which $\sqrt V$, hence the edit itself, decays: $\sqrt{V(G^{k}x)}\le \texttt{eff\_rate}^{\,k}\sqrt{V(x)}$.
The pendulum mnemonic (why the Euclidean norm misleads). A damped pendulum's kinetic energy peaks at the bottom of every swing even as friction bleeds the total energy monotonically to zero — kinetic and potential slosh back and forth while their sum only ever falls. Our non-normal $G$ does the same: the Euclidean norm $\|G^{k}x\|_2$ is like watching only the kinetic energy — it can grow for many hops (the transient hump, §1d) as the perturbation sloshes between directions — while the total adapted energy $V=\|x\|_P^{2}$ falls at every hop. The $P$-norm is the honest total-energy bookkeeping; the Euclidean norm is one sloshing component. Non-normality is precisely the sloshing (normal $G$ = no sloshing, the two norms coincide, no hump — §1g). The certificate's whole job is to stop measuring the kinetic component and measure the conserved-minus-dissipated total, in which the decay is monotone and the reach is just an energy budget: $\xi = 1/\ln(1/\texttt{eff\_rate}) = (\text{stored energy})\,/\,(\text{dissipation per hop})$. On the causal face the chain is finite and $Q$-drains $V$ to exactly zero within $\le(\#\text{windows})$ hops — a finite energy budget fully spent, no residual — which is the nilpotent corner and the product-Lyapunov form.

(i) Restricting $P$ to block-diagonal: a free sound directional charge, and the causal↔bidir unification

The dense Gramian $P$ gives one global rate and constant $\sqrt{\lambda_{\max}(P)}$, charged to every source, receiver and direction alike (§1e–f). To make the certificate directionally and per-source aware, restrict the metric to be block-diagonal by window, $P=\mathrm{blkdiag}(P_1,\dots,P_m)$, and read the Stein inequality as a feasibility problem $(G/r)^{*}P(G/r)\preceq P-I$. Two payoffs, one free and one structural.

The free half (no new solve). If an edit $\delta h$ is supported in window $j$, then for the D-preconditioned source $u=D^{-1}\delta h$ (also supported in $j$), $$ u^{*}Pu = u_j^{*}P_{jj}\,u_j \quad\text{EXACTLY} $$ — the off-diagonal blocks drop, because $u$ has a single nonzero block. So the diagonal block $P_{jj}$ of the already-cached dense Gramian delivers a certified, source-and-direction-anisotropic charge at zero extra cost, keeping the $d$-decay from $G$'s bandwidth: $$ \big\|(R\,\delta h)_{\text{far}}\big\|_2 \;\le\; \sqrt{u_j^{*}P_{jj}u_j}\;\frac{\texttt{eff\_rate}^{\,d_0}}{1-\texttt{eff\_rate}} \qquad[\textbf{sound}]. $$ This is the certified cousin of the C2d directional charge $\|F_p\,\delta h\|$ (the exact linear response — tight but not sound): it upper-bounds that exact response, so the tight-but-heuristic directional tier becomes sound. Measured (C2e, $\sim$180 edits over the bidir ladder, one certified rate per checkpoint):

  • Sound, universally: $\texttt{sound\_far}\ge\texttt{pred\_far}$ with 0 violations on every checkpoint (and $\texttt{global\_far}\ge\texttt{sound\_far}$ likewise).
  • Anisotropy is large and pays: per-window $\sqrt{\lambda_{\max}(P_{jj})}$ spans 5.5–150×; using $P_{jj}$ instead of the whole-matrix $\lambda_{\max}(P)$ tightens the sound bound by $\approx$5–22× (median $\sim$11×), while sitting only $\sim$6–20× above the exact response on well-conditioned cells.
  • It recovers the direction half of the taxonomy, not the reader half: the charge cleanly ranks filler (task-inert direction) 1–2 orders below the rest on every checkpoint, but does not reliably order irrelevant vs relevant (monotone on 3/8). That split is a reader/routing property the source block $P_{jj}$ is structurally blind to — which is exactly the boundary that hands that job to the reader-set/DWR bound. The complementarity is now measured, not asserted.
  • The certifiable rate tracks conditioning: the common rate rises from $r\!\approx\!0.55$ on well-conditioned cells to $0.90$–$0.95$ near-singular, and the stiffest cell (bidirnp40, $\sigma_{\min}=0.024$) admits no common rate on the grid at all — the bound is loose exactly where conditioning is bad, as it must be.
One certificate family, two solution modes. Block-diagonal Stein is not a new certificate — it is the family that contains both faces. On the causal face $G$ is strictly block-bidiagonal (subdiagonal transfers $T_i$; block size $=W$), and the block-diagonal Stein inequality decouples into a backward recursion with no SDP: $$ P_i = I + \tfrac{1}{r^{2}}\,T_i^{*}P_{i+1}T_i,\qquad P_m=I, $$ always feasible on a finite chain — and that recursion of per-window Gramians is the product-Lyapunov certificate of #9. On the bidirectional face $G$ is block-tridiagonal, the recursion does not close, and it is a genuine LMI (a modest chordal SDP at toy scale). So the keystone sharpens: one operator $G$, two regimes becomes one certificate family (block-diagonal Stein), two solution modes — backward recursion (nilpotent corner) vs LMI (contractive). The dense-$P$ Stein solve of §1e is the isotropic special case that discards the per-window structure both faces otherwise expose.

The catch (why we mine the free half first). Restricting $P$ to block-diagonal shrinks the feasible set, so a block-diagonal solution need not exist all the way down to $\rho(G)$ — the diagonal-stability / scaled-small-gain gap, real precisely when transient energy crosses several windows before dissipating. Expect the certifiable $r$ to rise and the constants to become anisotropic and mostly smaller. At the distances that matter here ($d=1$–3, where §3 shows the constant dominates the bound), anisotropic constants at a slightly worse rate should win — which is why the free diagonal-block charge above already captures most of the prize, and the full LMI is worth only a timeboxed check.

2. The scalar mnemonic that carries the shape

Strip the matrices away. Let $s_k=\|G^k\|$ be a scalar sequence that first grows then decays — the transient hump. The archetype is

$$ s_k = k\,(\tfrac12)^{k}: \qquad s_1=s_2=0.5,\; s_3=0.375,\; s_4=0.25,\;\dots \to 0 \text{ at rate } \rho=\tfrac12. $$

We cannot sum $\sum_{k\ge d}s_k$ in closed form, and $\rho^k=(\tfrac12)^k$ is not an upper bound (it misses the early bump). The move: dominate the bumpy sequence by a clean geometric one. Choose a rate $r$ above the true one — say $r=0.6$ — and take the smallest constant $C$ with $s_k\le C\,r^{k}$ for all $k$:

$$ C = \sup_k \frac{s_k}{r^k} = \sup_k\, k\Big(\tfrac{0.5}{0.6}\Big)^{k} \approx 2.0 \;(<\infty, \text{ since } s_k/r^k\to0). $$

The $\sup$ is finite precisely because $r>\rho$ forces $s_k/r^k\to0$, so the hump is a finite overshoot. Then the tail collapses to a geometric series:

$$ \sum_{k\ge d} s_k \;\le\; C\sum_{k\ge d} r^{k} \;=\; \frac{C}{1-r}\,r^{\,d}. $$

That is the bound's entire skeleton: $\dfrac{C}{1-r}\,r^{d}$. Compare with §1(f) — the matrix certificate is this exact expression, with two substitutions:

  • $r \to \texttt{eff\_rate}$  (the adapted norm delivers a base a hair below $r$, a free extra bit of contraction);
  • $C \to \sqrt{\kappa(P)}$  (we cannot take a $\sup$ over infinitely many matrix powers, so the Gramian computes the dominating constant in one Stein solve instead).

The mnemonic pins the rate at $r$ and dumps the whole hump into $C$; the Stein machine is the same bound, but able to trim the rate below $r$ (all the way to $\rho$ when $G$ is normal — §1g), paying instead in $\sqrt{\kappa(P)}$. For our strongly non-normal $G$ the trim is negligible, so $\texttt{eff\_rate}\approx r$ and the mnemonic's rate-$r$ picture is essentially what runs — which is why it is faithful, not a caricature.

3. The three-way play of $d$, $r$, and eff_rate

$d$ — block-distance (given by the question)
How many window-hops separate positions $i$ and $j$. You do not choose it; it is the reach you are asking about. The bound decays as $\texttt{eff\_rate}^{\,d}$.
$r$ — target rate (a free knob you tune)
The rate you ask the Stein solve to certify. Legal for any $r\in(\rho(G),1)$. It is pure scaffolding: it selects which Gramian $P$ you build, and then disappears from the final bound. Push $r\downarrow\rho(G)$ for a steeper envelope but a blowing-up constant ($\sqrt{\kappa(P)}\to\infty$); raise $r$ for a gentle envelope with a small constant.
eff_rate $=r\,c_P$ — the certified base (an output, not a choice)
What the adapted norm actually delivers, below $r$ by the Stein drain (§1g): $=\rho(G)$ when $G$ is normal, $\approx r$ when strongly non-normal. This — not $r$, not $\rho(G)$ — is the number raised to the $d$. Bracketed by $\rho(G)\le\texttt{eff\_rate}<1$: you can never certify below the true asymptotic rate.
$\rho(G)$ — the asymptotic floor (a fact, never in the formula)
The genuine decay rate. It sets the ideal reach $1/\ln(1/\rho(G))$ that eff_rate approaches from above as $r\downarrow\rho(G)$; the certified reach is that ideal plus the margin you pay for a finite constant.

The tradeoff, in real numbers (bidir16, $\rho(G)=0.332$; prefactor $=\sqrt{\kappa(P)}/(1-\texttt{eff\_rate})$, dropping the common $\|D^{-1}\|$). Read down each column — the best (smallest) bound is highlighted:

The optimal $r$ slides with the distance you are asking about:

  • Up close ($d=1$): $\texttt{eff\_rate}^{d}$ barely differs between rows, so the constant dominates — the gentle rate $r=0.48$ (small prefactor) wins.
  • Far out ($d=3,5$): a smaller base compounds exponentially, so the tight rate $r=0.38$ wins despite its larger constant — steepness beats the prefactor at distance.

You need not commit: the lower envelope $B^\star(d)=\min_r B(r,d)$ (best row per column) is itself a valid certified bound, and it automatically does the right thing — loose near the edit, tightening toward $\rho(G)$ as you look further out.

Why eff_rate is not "a posteriori cheating." $r$ and $P$ are chosen from the operator $G$ alone (the linearization at the fixed point) — no edit, no measured response is used. The Gramian just computes the dominating constant that the scalar mnemonic would get by a $\sup$. The bound is a genuine a-priori envelope; the only "posterior" object in the whole program is the separate deployable certificate $\|z-z^\*\|\le\|f(z)-z\|/\sigma_{\min}$ (the residual bound, §4), which uses the actual residual.

4. The other certificate: the a-posteriori residual bound

Everything so far (§§1–3) is a priori: it bounds the resolvent from the operator $M=I-J$ alone, answering where an edit can reach. There is a second, complementary certificate — the one you actually deploy at solve time — that turns $\sigma_{\min}$ into a hard stopping guarantee. It is a single inequality:

$$ \boxed{\;\|z - z^{\star}\| \;\le\; \frac{\|f(z)-z\|}{\sigma_{\min}(I-J)}.\;} $$

At any iterate $z$ during a solve, the residual $f(z)-z$ is something you compute for free (one function eval — you were going to apply $f$ anyway). The bound says the true distance to the equilibrium $z^{\star}$ is that residual amplified by $1/\sigma_{\min}$.

Where it comes from

Let $g(z)=f(z)-z$, so the equilibrium is its root, $g(z^{\star})=0$. The fundamental theorem of calculus along the segment from $z^{\star}$ to $z$ gives

$$ g(z) = g(z)-g(z^{\star}) = \Big[\underbrace{\textstyle\int_0^1\!\big(J(w_t)-I\big)\,dt}_{-A}\Big](z-z^{\star}), \qquad w_t = z^{\star} + t(z-z^{\star}), $$

where $A=\int_0^1 (I-J(w_t))\,dt$ is the segment-averaged $(I-J)$. Invert and take norms: $z-z^{\star}=-A^{-1}g(z)$, so $\|z-z^{\star}\|\le\|A^{-1}\|\,\|f(z)-z\|=\|f(z)-z\|/\sigma_{\min}(A)$. Near the fixed point $A\approx I-J$ (exactly, if $f$ is affine) — the stated bound.

Why it is a certificate, not "residual looks small, stop"

Solvers habitually stop when the residual is small, silently assuming residual $\approx$ error. This bound makes the hidden amplifier explicit: $\text{error}=\text{residual}\times1/\sigma_{\min}$. Near singularity ($\sigma_{\min}\to0$) a tiny residual can still hide a large error — the naive rule fails exactly where conditioning bites, and this one does not (it inflates the tolerance accordingly). When $(I-J)$ is well-conditioned the same bound certifies the residual is a faithful proxy. Either way it is a proof that $z$ lies within a known tolerance of $z^{\star}$, not a hope.

The two halves. The a-priori reach envelope (§§1–3, rate $\rho(G)$/eff_rate) tells you where to recompute after an edit; this a-posteriori residual bound (amplifier $\sigma_{\min}$) tells you when you have recomputed enough — take the residual over the whole state and the untouched region is certified within tolerance. Cost is essentially nil: the residual is a byproduct of the solve, and $\sigma_{\min}(I-J)$ is the single number the program already estimates once at $z^{\star}$. It is also the part that is genuinely equilibrium-native: a feedforward stack has no fixed-point residual to self-check against.

The rigorous version (Newton–Kantorovich), and where $L$ comes from

The stated bound is first-order: it uses $\sigma_{\min}(I-J)$ in place of the segment-averaged $\sigma_{\min}(A)$ — exact for affine $f$, good near $z^{\star}$. To make it a theorem at finite curvature you must control how fast $J$ varies: a Lipschitz constant $L$ with $\|J(u)-J(v)\|\le L\|u-v\|$, i.e. a bound on $f$'s second derivative $\|\partial^{2}f\|$. With $\beta=1/\sigma_{\min}(I-J)$ at the iterate, $\eta=\beta\|f(z)-z\|$, and $h=\beta L\eta$, Newton–Kantorovich gives: if $h\le\tfrac12$ a unique $z^{\star}$ exists within

$$ \|z-z^{\star}\|\;\le\;R_-=\frac{1-\sqrt{1-2h}}{\beta L}. $$

As $h\to0$ (well-conditioned, small residual, mild curvature) $R_-\to\eta=\|f(z)-z\|/\sigma_{\min}$ — the linear bound is exactly its limit; when $h>\tfrac12$ there is no valid radius and the certificate abstains rather than lying.

Where $L$ comes from — one new ingredient (a curvature bound on $f$), on a rigor↔cost spectrum:

  • Analytic: compose it from the weight operator norms and the curvature moduli of softmax and the activation (GELU) — fully rigorous but pessimistic (the usual transformer-Lipschitz looseness).
  • Probed: $L$ is only needed on the ball, so sample $\|J(z+\delta)-J(z)\|/\|\delta\|$ at a few random $\delta$ of size $\sim R_-$ (each one Hessian–vector product via autodiff, cheap) and take the max — tight, but an estimate, so the certificate is rigorous only conditional on it.
  • Certified-local: an interval / CROWN-style bound on $\|\partial^{2}f\|$ over the ball — rigorous and toy-feasible; the honest "real theorem" path.
Why a crude $L$ is fine — but only near convergence. $L$ enters only through $h=\beta L\eta$, and $\eta$ (the residual-scaled error) is tiny near convergence — so even a loose $L$ clears $h\le\tfrac12$ comfortably there; $\eta$ does the work, $L$ only has to be finite. The catch we since measured: the one-shot warm-start (or even Woodbury-predicted) init is not near convergence on ill-conditioned substrates ($h\gg\tfrac12$; see the trilemma below), so NK is a certified stopping rule — iterate the re-solve, then certify — not a certificate on the warm init itself.

Note. This replaces the earlier hand-wave ("an $\inf$ of $\sigma_{\min}$ over a Kantorovich-type neighborhood"): $R_-$ is that neighborhood, made concrete. It remains an owed experiment (measure $L$, report $h$ and $R_-$ on the trained substrates), not a gap in the argument.

What we measured — and the tightness trilemma

Run this on the trained substrates with real post-edit residuals ($r=f_{\text{new}}(z^{\star}_{\text{old}})-z^{\star}_{\text{old}}$, the residual a warm re-solve actually starts from) and the clean story sharpens into an honest one. The residual bound is not one object but three, and each surrenders exactly one of {cheap, tight, rigorous}:

objectwhat it ischeap?tight (real edits)?rigorous?
scalar $\;\|r\|/\sigma_{\min}$worst-case amplification✓✗  loose 7–34×✓ over-estimate
directional $\;\|R\,r\|$first-order actual error $M^{-1}r$~ needs resolvent✓ ~1× (small edits)✗ under-predicts 0.66×

Why the scalar is loose. The real post-edit residual lives in the fast modes — its measured alignment with the stiff subspace (the small-$\sigma$ directions $M^{-1}$ amplifies) is only $\approx0.05$. Charging every residual the worst-case $1/\sigma_{\min}$ therefore over-counts by the stiff/fast spread — here 7–34×. Why the directional is tight but not safe. $\|R\,r\|$ uses the true per-mode amplification and is essentially exact on small edits ($0.98$–$1.01$, 10–34× tighter than scalar) — but on large edits it turns optimistic, under-predicting to $0.66\times$ as nonlinearity and cached-resolvent drift ($R_{\text{old}}\ne R_{\text{new}}$) bite. It is a tight estimate, not a guaranteed bound. Why NK is narrow. $h=\beta^{2}L\|r\|\le\tfrac12$ needs $\|r\|\lesssim1/(2\beta^{2}L)$; with $\beta=1/\sigma_{\min}\approx18$–$41$ that is near-convergence, and none of the one-shot post-edit (or Woodbury-predicted) residuals cleared it.

The impossible triangle — cheap + tight + rigorous, pick two (measured, not asserted). The tight deployable object (directional) is effectively a heuristic; the rigorous one (NK) fires only near convergence, as a stopping rule. The one principled route to sound-and-tighter is on a different axis: a reader-set / goal-oriented bound (adjoint / dual-weighted-residual) that measures the residual's effect only on the output at reader positions, dropping the far-field slack that makes the scalar loose — still an over-estimate (sound), but tighter. That is the reader-set principle in a-posteriori-estimation clothing, and the open lever for a certificate that is both rigorous and not loose.

5. Two invariants, kept straight

The certificate touches two distinct pseudospectral facts, and the paper's title lives on keeping them apart:

  • $\rho(G)$ governs SHAPE / reach — how far a perturbation travels before it dies (the $\texttt{eff\_rate}^{d}$ envelope above).
  • $\sigma_{\min}(I-J)$ governs SCALE / amplitude — $\|M^{-1}\|=1/\sigma_{\min}$, the height of the response and the error-amplification $\text{resid}/\sigma_{\min}$.

Neither is $\rho(J)$ (temporal contraction, which may exceed 1 with no harm). "Conditioning, not contraction" is precisely this: reach and amplitude are set by $\rho(G)$ and $\sigma_{\min}$, not by whether the map contracts.
