C7: Maintained Context, and the Predict–Certify–Correct Loop

Zhibai Jia · 15 July 2026 · one cached certificate, a stream of edits, and the question — does cheap maintenance stay sound (never over-claims locality) and cheap (fewer solves than starting over)? The design, the maintenance-cost ladder, what the equilibrium predictor buys, three honest negatives, two bugs found and killed, and the control-theoretic reading.

Scope. Notes #11/#12 give the static certificates (value edits, the reach envelope, structural bordering). C7 is the dynamic companion: it validates that those certificates are closed under a stream of edits against a single cached base. Everything is toy-scale (a $d{=}64$ sliding-window DEQ transformer on controlled-gap MQAR), oracle-instrumented (dense Jacobians, exact resolvents), and the efficiency numbers are solver f-evaluations, not wall-clock (the substrate is launch/CPU-bound). Clean, amplitude-corrected ladder results exist for bidirnp24, bidirnp40, curr24; the anchored long cells are preliminary (earlier code).

1. What C7 is

Take one trained model on one context. Build a certificate cache once. Then throw a stream of edits at it and ask two questions at every step: is the cheap maintenance machinery still sound (it never certifies a locality it does not have) and still cheap (fewer solver iterations than redoing the equilibrium from scratch)? The loop is predict → certify → correct — numerical continuation (Euler–Newton) with an error bar deciding whether the corrector is even needed.

The organizing idea is a taxonomy of reuse: everything you cache and reuse after an edit falls into three families, each with its own condition for when reuse stops being safe.

The punchline of the taxonomy. Reuse is cheap; knowing when to stop reusing is what the certificate buys. Each family fails independently — the state can be stale while the operator is fine, both fine while the certificate's budget is spent — which is exactly why the loop needs three separate gates, not one global freshness check.

2. The loop, object by object

Phase 0 — build the cache (once)

Solve the equilibrium; then compute everything the loop may reuse: the resolvent $R$; the conditioning $\sigma_{\min}(I-J)$; the block-Jacobi transport operator $G$ and its Stein/Lyapunov Gramian $P$ (the expensive object — an early-stopped sum $\sum_k (G/r)^{\top k}(G/r)^k$, the thing we most want to avoid recomputing); the certified rate $r$ and adapted-norm amplitude; and the rank-8 carry subspace the responses actually live in. This is the amortization bet: pay once, reuse many times.

Phase 1 — single edits, validate each object

For each edit kind — insert, delete, value-substitution — at varied positions:

  • The gate — one scalar ($\sigma_{\min}(S)$ per insert, $\|R_{bb}^{-1}\|$ per delete) that flags fold-risk. It must vary by position (a hub token is riskier than filler) — that variation is its validation.
  • The envelope — the a-priori locality certificate $\|[R]_{ij}\|\le C\,\rho^{d(i,j)}$: check it never under-predicts the measured response (soundness) and by how much it over-predicts (looseness).
  • The predictor — instead of re-solving, guess the new equilibrium ($z^\star_\text{old}+R\,\delta h$ for a value edit; a bordered-Newton step for an insert), then correct. Count solver iterations from cold / warm / predicted starts.

Phase 2 — an edit stream, cache maintained

Fire many churn rounds against the maintained cache and watch the certificate go stale. A running budget tracks operator drift; when spent, the certificate is recapitalized (rebuild $P$). We measure edits-per-recert — the whole amortization story — and confirm the drift bound is never violated before the recert fires.

4. What the predictor buys (measured)

The single most-quoted number: warm-starting the corrector from the tangent/Woodbury prediction versus the stale cache, in iterations-to-tolerance. The predictor is stellar for value edits, useful for inserts, and the honest scaling law is win $\propto L/\xi$ — the fraction of the sequence already converged at warm init. It pays on long contexts with short reach; it cannot pay at toy $L\sim 2w$ where the ball fills the sequence.

5. The certificate holds — soundness and the ride-along

After the amplitude fix (§7), every envelope verdict is sound on the geometric cells (insert / delete / subst all pass on bidirnp24 and bidirnp40); the nilpotent causal cell (curr24) correctly abstains (its certificate is the exact product form, not the geometric envelope — the system declining to certify is a feature). The budget theorem holds in every round: measured staleness stays under the predicted bound, often nearly tight.

The prettiest side-result. The Stein Gramian's top eigenspace aligns with the carry subspace — principal cosines [0.91, 0.72, 0.66, …] (bidirnp24), [0.89, 0.85, 0.55, …] (bidirnp40). The directions the certificate weights most heavily (where transient amplification lives) are the directions the edit response actually rides. First evidence that the two invariants — $\sigma_{\min}$ (amplitude) and $\rho(G)$ (reach) — are two readings of one low-rank memory channel, not merely coupled scalars.

6. Three honest negatives

  1. The cheap accept-gate is not perfectly sound. R3 accepts the predictor when $\|r\|/\sigma_{\min}<\tau$ — a tier-2 estimate, not a rigorous enclosure. It evaluates $\sigma_{\min}$ at the base point and assumes it holds along the path to $z^\star$; where the map curves, the true error exceeds it. Measured: ~1 in 45 edits mis-accepts (a bounded ~1.5$\tau$ overrun, not a blow-up). Fine as a heuristic; for a rigorous claim it needs Newton–Kantorovich (rigorous but conservative, needs the curvature $L$) or a $\kappa$ safety factor or verify-after-accept. This is the impossible triangle made concrete.
  2. The directional charge does not fix R3. We ported the sound block-diagonal Stein charge $\sqrt{u^\top P_{jj}u}/(1-\text{eff})$ into the accept-gate (it is provably sound and tighter than $\|r\|/\sigma_{\min}$ on near-singular cells). Result: efficiency unchanged (3.74× vs 3.73×) and soundness worse (unsound 1→2 on bidirnp24). Reason: R3's gap is nonlinear, and a tighter linear bound just admits more borderline-nonlinear predicts. R3's regression is also structural — subst already solves in ~1 eval, so gating it saves nothing; R3 pays only at high $L/\xi$, never from a tighter bound. Directional's proper home is the envelope (a-priori, sound by construction), not the gate.
  3. Full recapitalization is astronomical. The pessimistic recap (rebuild $P$ from scratch on the drifted operator) costs ~28.7M f-eval-equivalents — versus 1808 to just cold-solve all 45 edits, i.e. ~15,000× the cost of not maintaining at all. This is not a bug; it is why the low-rank Stein update (rank-$O(d)$ RHS at the cut) is essential, not optional. The full rebuild is a non-starter; the budget exists precisely to avoid paying it every edit, and the deferred low-rank recap is what makes the maintenance regime viable.

Two further honest scoping points: the envelope's tightness is ~$10^5$ (the genuine a-priori Stein looseness — a worst-case transient constant; the anisotropic per-window bound is what tightens it, and it lives in the envelope, not the gate); and the recapitalization can fail — if the drifted operator admits no valid rate, the loop bails to a full re-solve (an abstention data point, distinct from a routine recert).

7. Two bugs found and killed (the method matters)

The envelope initially reported violations. Both causes were mine, not the theory's — and both surfaced only because more samples were run:

  • Dropped amplitude factor. The certified resolvent amplitude is $C=\sqrt{\lambda_{\max}(P)}\cdot\|D^{-1}\|/(1-r)$; the code used $\sqrt{\lambda_{\max}(P)}$ alone. The missing $\|D^{-1}\|/(1-r)$ (48× on bidirnp24, 146× on the stiffer bidirnp40) made the bound too small — hence the "violations." Fixed, verified: all verdicts sound.
  • Hop-distance binning. Verdicts used $\lceil |i-\text{cut}|/w\rceil$, which can overstate block distance by one across a window boundary, shrinking the bound below the certificate. Switched to the true block distance $|i/w-\text{cut}/w|$ — correct for verdicts (the ceil convention is fine for slope fits, unsound for verdicts).

Lesson worth keeping: an envelope "violation" on a sound theorem is almost always a dropped constant or a units slip. Verify the constant before doubting the math.

8. The control-theoretic reading

The flavor is earned where it is technical. The Lyapunov/Stein certificate is the actual contribution — quarantining non-normality into $\sqrt{\kappa(P)}$ via an adapted norm is straight observability-Gramian machinery, and it certifies transient-heavy reach that approximation theory could not. The pretty correspondence: $\sigma_{\min}(I-J)$ is a resolvent norm at a point, $\rho(G)$ a reach/decay rate, and $P$'s top eigenspace is the dominant controllable-observable subspace — so §5's alignment is a control-theoretic statement.

But it is numerical control, not feedback control. The lineage is Lyapunov certificates + model-order-reduction Gramians + numerical continuation (Deuflhard/Keller predict–correct, adaptive re-identification on recap) — not $H_\infty$/robust margins. And the "dynamics" is subtle: the Lyapunov object certifies spatial transport (block-Jacobi reach), and the "sequence" is the edit stream, not time; temporal contraction $\rho(J)$ is deliberately a non-participant. The "budget/margin" is a Lyapunov-residual staleness meter, not a gain/phase margin. Keep the flavor, scope it: we borrow the certificate and the loop, we do not claim control guarantees.

9. What is not done

  • Directional in the envelope (its proper home): tightens the ~$10^5$ reach looseness and would make the anchor content-dependent-locality split visible (filler certified local, bindings ride the hub). Efficiency stays latent — it only cashes out via a localized re-solver (solve the certified ball, freeze the rest); the current corrector solves jointly.
  • Low-rank recapitalization (R6): the rank-$O(d)$ Stein update that beats the 28.7M-f-eval full rebuild.
  • DWR reader-set in the loop: goal-oriented skips (recompute an answer only if a reader is reached); the substrate supports it (query tokens are the readers) but it is a separate cached-adjoint port.
  • Longer $L$ for R3: the gate's payoff is an $L/\xi$-scaling claim; a clean demonstration needs long-context-few-edits, which toy $L$ cannot show — honest hand-waving to scale is, for now, the correct call.
  • Clean anchored-cell ladder: the long anchored substrates ($\sigma_{\min}\sim0.015$, the first cells with length and conditioning) need a full amplitude-corrected run.
Net. The certificate system is closed under context edits, and C7 measures it: gates vary by position, the envelope is sound (once the constant is right), the equilibrium predictor captures essentially all the toy-scale efficiency (~3–4.5× fewer solver iterations), and the budget theorem holds soundly across a churn stream. The negatives are as informative as the wins — the cheap gate's nonlinear unsoundness, directional's misfit for the gate, and the recapitalization bill that names the one optimization (low-rank $P$-update) the maintenance regime actually needs.
