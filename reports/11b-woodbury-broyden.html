The Woodbury Prior and the Anderson→Broyden Swap

Zhibai Jia · 9 July 2026 · what the low-rank corridor buys you at solve time — predict the new equilibrium, certify it, and (crucially) pick the solver that can cash a good init

Scope. This is the efficiency spin-off, not the paper's spine. The spine is a characterization (two invariants, the reach envelope, the residual certificate, the reader-set). This note records the one constructive payoff of the low-rank corridor — a predict→certify→correct maintenance loop — and the solver fact we bumped into on the way. Everything here is toy-scale; wall-clock is disclaimed.

1. The idea: edit the resolvent, predict the equilibrium

An in-context edit changes one token, hence the injection $h\to h+\delta h$ ($\delta h$ localized). We do not edit the equilibrium state directly — it is a derived fixed point. We predict where it moves. Differentiating the fixed-point condition $z^\star=f(z^\star,h)$ gives the linear response through the resolvent $M^{-1}=(I-J)^{-1}$:

$$ \delta z^{\star} = (I-J)^{-1}\,\delta h, \qquad z_{\text{init}} = z^{\star}_{\text{old}} + (I-J)^{-1}\delta h. $$

That "warmer-than-warm" start is the old equilibrium plus its predicted first-order move — exact if $f$ were affine, then residual-certified (Note #11, §4) and corrected only if needed. Kantorovich in three beats: resolvent = predictor, residual bound = the "do I even iterate?" test, solver = corrector.

2. Why Woodbury, and how it avoids materializing $M^{-1}$

Two low-rank objects hide here; keep them apart. The displacement field $\delta z$ over the $(L,d)$ grid is itself rank $\sim8$ (the carry subspace, C2d-V4) — you can truncate it. Separately, the operator change $\delta J = UV^{\top}$ is low-rank because the edit is localized, and Woodbury updates the inverse:

$$ M_{\text{new}}^{-1} = M_{\text{old}}^{-1} + M_{\text{old}}^{-1}U\,(I - V^{\top}M_{\text{old}}^{-1}U)^{-1}\,V^{\top}M_{\text{old}}^{-1}. $$

You never form $M^{-1}$ as a matrix. You need its action on $r{+}1$ vectors:

  • $a = M_{\text{old}}^{-1}\delta h$  (one solve), and $W = M_{\text{old}}^{-1}U$  ($r\approx8$ solves → a tall-skinny $N\times r$, not $N\times N$);
  • each solve of $(I-J_{\text{old}})x=b$ is matrix-free — $(I-J)$ applied as a JVP through $f$, and it is exactly the linear system DEQ implicit-diff already solves for the backward pass, so the machinery exists (torchdeq's b_solver);
  • the only inverse you form is the tiny $r\times r$ block $(I-V^{\top}W)$.
Blocked structure makes it contiguous. The band structure of $J$ gives (Demko–Moss–Smith) a resolvent whose blocks decay with distance, so the cached $M^{-1}$ is low-bandwidth and its action is a banded op; the edit footprint is a few contiguous windows; the correction decays and truncates to the reach ball. Everything is block/window-contiguous and low-rank — dense BLAS, never per-token gather. Cost per edit is $O(\text{ball}\times r)$, independent of sequence length. It transfers to any DEQ with (i) a cheap $M^{-1}$ action (banded / hierarchical / block-sparse) and (ii) a low-rank edit — i.e. a spatially local edit. A diffuse-but-sparse $\delta J$ is sparse-but-high-rank and gets no help. (Deployable form not yet built — our toy runs used the dense oracle $R$ for exactness.)

3. What it buys — measured

Accuracy: a clean win. The Woodbury init lands strictly closer than plain warm-start — 14/15 cells, 1.8–65× lower residual (C5), and the low-rank truncation keeps it. On the real post-edit residuals it cut $\|r\|$ by 18–39× (e.g. curr08 filler $5.47\to0.31$).

Efficiency: the solver decides whether the win cashes. Under Anderson, a lower-residual init did not mean fewer iterations — residual $\ne$ iterations (curr16 filler: 8× lower residual, yet more Anderson iters). Anderson's convergence tracks the slow/stiff-mode content, not the initial residual. The fix is the corrector: swap to Broyden.

4. The solver lesson: Anderson vs Broyden

Both are quasi-Newton (Anderson acceleration is a multi-secant Broyden-type method — so this is not a Picard-vs-Newton story). They differ in stability near stiff fixed points: Anderson's internal least-squares ill-conditions as $\sigma_{\min}\to0$ (a known failure mode; the textbook remedy is to regularize the least-squares), and in practice it floors around $10^{-4}$. At an achievable tolerance the swap pays decisively on exactly the near-singular operators that are our subject:

Broyden converges 100% everywhere, including the near-singular cells where Anderson stalls — ~5–10× fewer f-evals and actual convergence. And within Broyden the Woodbury init then trims a further ~15–30% (up to 2×), so the prior finally converts to iterations.

It is regime-dependent — our own two datapoints straddle it. Earlier this campaign Broyden NaN'd on our set/graph DEQs — but those forced strong contraction ($\rho$ pinned $\sim0.9$), giving a degenerate residual history for the quasi-Newton update. The transformer substrate here is noncontractive-but-well-conditioned — the regime we advocate ("conditioning, not contraction") — and it is exactly the regime where Broyden shines and solver choice bites hardest. So: no universal winner; the design philosophy and the solver are coupled. The field default is actually Anderson (cheaper) — our preference for Broyden is specific to stiff/near-singular operators at tight tolerance.
What it does not touch. The characterization — $\sigma_{\min}$, $\rho(G)$, the reach envelope, the noncontractive witnesses — is computed from the Jacobian at a Newton-polished fixed point via dense linear algebra, so it is solver-independent. We checked: Broyden and Anderson land on the same fixed point and reproduce $\sigma_{\min}$, $\rho(J)$, and the resolvent to 6+ digits on the witness cells (one exception, curr40, is genuinely multistable — two branches, and each is independently well-posed, which is fine for a local maintenance certificate).

5. Novelty and honest placement

The Woodbury/Sherman–Morrison identity is generic (Kalman, K-FAC, ROME's key-covariance edit, influence functions, delta-rule state updates). What is DEQ-native is the target: Woodbury updates an inverse, and the edit-response is an inverse — the resolvent $(I-J)^{-1}$ — only because there is a fixed point. A feedforward stack pushes a perturbation forward (a JVP); there is no inverse to update and no iterate to warm. The specific cross-edit prior (measure the low-rank footprint → update the resolvent → predict → residual- certify) we did not find in the DEQ literature (verify with Geng before any novelty claim). Placement: a constructive corollary of the low-rank corridor — an appendix that shows the characterization is exploitable — pitched at the efficiency spin-off, not the paper's spine.
