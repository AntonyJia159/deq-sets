Set-DEQ Notes · #3

Hunting the Unlearning Leak: Jacobians, Multistability, and a Null Result

Zhibai Jia · 20 June 2026 · working notes, not for circulation beyond reviewers

1. The question we set out to answer

Notes #1 and #2 established a clean picture on a synthetic cluster-counting task: a contractive update (mean-pool with spectral norm or LayerNorm) has an exactly unique fixed point — and therefore exact path independence and exact unlearning — while the more expressive attention update buys accuracy at the cost of a residual non-uniqueness, and the properties survive an 8× change in set size. The open thread was a worst-case tail: occasionally, removing a point and re-solving from the previous state (a warm start) lands somewhere different from a cold solve. This note asks the question that decides whether that tail matters: is the residual leak actually exploitable by an attacker — and if not, what is the multistability doing?

The short version: we built sharper and sharper instruments to find the leak, and the honest answer is that the warm-start channel is benign. This is a negative result, reported as such. Below is the road to it, because the road has the content.

2. The Jacobian probe: local contraction is necessary but not sufficient

At a converged fixed point Z* the relevant object is the update Jacobian J_f = ∂f/∂Z. Its spectral radius ρ(J_f) is the local well-posedness number: ρ < 1 means Z* is a local attractor. We estimate it matrix-free, by power iteration on Jacobian-vector products (reverse-over-reverse, so it works through attention too).

Attention0.78 / 0.92100% 0.06–0.70.73 (29/30)

The result refutes the tidy story we were about to tell. Attention is locally contractive at every fixed point it reaches (ρ < 1, max 0.92) and yet carries a large worst-case unlearning gap that survives restricting to cases where both the warm and cold solves genuinely converged (0.73, 29/30). So the leak is not a local instability: it is multistability — several coexisting locally-stable basins.

Why this is not a contradiction. A fixed-point iteration only ever lands on attractors, and every attractor necessarily has ρ < 1 — so measuring ρ < 1 at the points the solver reaches is almost tautological. The informative quantity is the number of distinct attractors, which the local Jacobian cannot see. Picture an energy landscape with several valleys: each valley floor is locally attracting, but there are several. The diagnostic therefore needs two coordinates:
  • local ρ(J_f) < 1 and global fp_gap = 0 → a single global attractor (mean-pool);
  • local ρ(J_f) < 1 but fp_gap > 0 → multistable (attention).

This also matches theory rather than contradicting it: the Ramsauer et al. (2020) correspondence says attention is the update of a modern Hopfield network, and Hopfield networks are multistable by design — each stored pattern is an attractor. Attention being multistable is the mechanism working as intended, not a pathology.

3. Two attempts to engineer the multistability away

3a. Decomposability (linear attention) — inconclusive

One hypothesis: attention is multistable because its aggregate is not a simple sufficient statistic. Linear attention (φ(q)·∑_j φ(k_j) v_jᵀ) is an additive sufficient statistic — decomposable — yet more expressive than mean-pool. If decomposability were the lever, it should be unique.

It was not decisive. Linear attention failed to train competitively on this task (accuracy 0.52–0.57, below mean-pool — linear attention is known to be weak where sharp selection is needed), and once it did converge it was genuinely multistable (both-converged gap 0.34). So a decomposable model was multistable too: the lean shifts toward expressiveness, not decomposability, as the driver — but the poor training and high single-seed variance forbid a firm claim. We logged it as inconclusive rather than forcing a narrative.

3b. Trainable path independence (Anil et al. 2022) — tames the warm-start channel

A better idea, from Path Independent Equilibrium Models Can Better Exploit Test-Time Computation: path independence can be promoted by training on unrestricted (non-contractive) architectures — mixed zero/noise initialization plus a randomized solver budget — with no contraction constraint. Their adversarial-initialization stress test is, read our way, exactly the warm-start channel: a warm start is a history-dependent initialization. We applied the recipe to attention and measured it over 10 paired seeds (same data and seed per pair; only the recipe differs).

Three things, stated at the precision the seeds support:

  • No accuracy cost (0.833 vs 0.834). An earlier single-seed run had shown an accuracy gain — that was noise.
  • The worst-case warm-start gap drops, and the effect is real but only via the paired design. Unpaired the error bars overlap, but per seed the PI-recipe is ≤ baseline in all 10 seeds, strictly lower in 7 (paired t ≈ 3.2, p ≈ 0.01).
  • Global multistability is not fixed (fp_gap unchanged). The recipe tames the operating-region (warm-start) channel specifically — ambiguous cases now fail to converge rather than silently settling in a wrong basin, a safer failure mode — without making the map globally single-basin.

A methodological aside worth keeping: the paper's own Asymptotic-Alignment (cosine) score was saturated and useless here (0.995 vs 0.997) — cosine washes out the magnitude differences between basins that unlearning cares about. The both-converged relative-norm gap is the metric that discriminates.

4. The decisive test: membership inference

A latent gap is only a leak if an attacker can use it. We built a membership-inference attack with a paired construction so that perfect unlearning gives AUC 0.5 by construction:

base survivors B (23 points), candidates c, d from B's own clusters
MEMBER     (label 1): solve B∪{c}; warm-delete c → Z_post; show (Z_post, c)
NON-MEMBER (label 0): solve B∪{d}; warm-delete d → Z_post; show (Z_post, c)

Same survivors, same presented candidate c; the only difference is whether c or d passed through — i.e. the warm-start history. A classifier (strong, latent-access, and a weaker readout-only variant) is trained to recover the label. 3 architectures × 5 seeds × 3000 trials. Because the leak is a tail phenomenon, we stratify by the per-trial signal magnitude ∥Z_postmember − Z_postnon-member∥ and report AUC restricted to the highest-gap trials.

A bug worth recording. A first pass split train/test per sample. Because a trial's member and non-member share the same survivors and candidate (near-identical inputs, opposite labels), the attacker memorized one twin and was fooled by the other, producing a systematic AUC below 0.5 across every config — the signature of contamination, not signal. Splitting by trial (both twins on the same side) restored the control to exactly 0.500.

Null result. The attacker gets no edge — in any stratum, for any model, including the worst-decile tail. And this is not a weak-attacker artifact: the same attacker on the unique-by-construction control bounds its capability, and the multistable baseline never beats the control (top-10%: 0.504 vs 0.512). If there were latent signal being missed, the baseline would exceed the floor; it does not. The rising spread as the strata shrink (0.001 → 0.017) is small-sample noise, not signal.

5. What the multistability actually does — and doesn't

Putting the instruments together: attention's multistability is real in latent-norm terms (the both-converged gap is genuine; two distinct fixed points are exhibited), but the basin a warm start lands in does not encode the identity of the deleted point in any recoverable way. Basin steering is orthogonal to membership. So the worry that powered this whole investigation — that warm-start unlearning leaks — is, on this task, unfounded.

Two consequences worth stating precisely:

  • Exact unlearning is sufficient but not necessary for membership safety. The expressive, non-unique regime is empirically safe without being exact.
  • Distance-to-exact does not predict risk. The both-converged gap — the natural “unlearning quality” metric, and the kind of closeness-to-retrain proxy the approximate-unlearning literature relies on — is decoupled from actual membership exploitability here: large gap, zero leakage.

6. Scope, and where this leaves things

We tested the history / warm-start channel — an architectural, dynamical property — and found it benign. The paired same-survivors design deliberately cancels the other channel: the data-intrinsic leak, where a deleted point is recoverable from its neighbors even after perfect deletion. That channel is statistical and context-dependent — on a Gaussian-mixture task it is semantically vacuous (a point’s “neighbors” are blob-mates; “a point was near here” is already implied) — so it is out of scope for an architectural study and would need data carrying genuine private attributes to be meaningful.

Honest headline. For equilibrium set-models, the multistability that expressive (attention) updates introduce is benign for warm-start unlearning: it produces no attacker-exploitable membership leak, even in the worst-case tail, even though it is genuine non-uniqueness. Trainable path independence (Anil et al.) shrinks the worst-case warm-start gap at no accuracy cost, but its real payoff is test-time scaling, not privacy — there is no leak for it to close. Exact unlearning remains available, trivially, for strict applications via a contractive (monotone-operator) cell.

All numbers are from a single synthetic task and small models on a laptop GPU; they are indicative, not a benchmark. The remaining belt-and-suspenders check is to escalate the attacker to a cross-attention model before treating the null as final — the control comparison already argues against a weak-attacker explanation.
