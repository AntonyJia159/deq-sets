Structured Edits: Insert/Delete as Operator Bordering

Zhibai Jia · 15 July 2026 · a value edit is a vector the resolvent eats; an insert changes the operator's size — the native language is matrix bordering + Schur complement. The full predictor derivation, delete as its dual, keeping the band exact by re-blocking, how far the shadow reaches (measured), and why the cheap adjoint is not rigorous.

Scope. Note #11 covers value (fixed-size) edits: $\delta z=(I-J)^{-1}\delta h$, the reach envelope, the residual and reader-set certificates. This is the structural companion — insert, delete, splice — which change the operator's dimension. Three registers, kept apart: the algebra (bordering/Schur, exact for the frozen operator), the measured shadow decay and rank, and one measured negative (no cheap rigorous adjoint). The warm-start / RAG payoffs are proposals pending C7; everything is toy-scale.

1. Why a structural edit is different

A value edit changes the injection in a fixed space, $h\to h+\delta h$; the operator $M=(I-J)$ keeps its shape and the resolvent eats the perturbation, $\delta z^\star=M^{-1}\delta h$. An insert or delete changes the sequence length, so $M$ changes size and every downstream index shifts. Under an absolute positional embedding that re-indexing is itself a dense edit (every shifted token's $h_0$ changes). Under a relative substrate (NO_POSW) a global shift is invisible to attention except at the cut, so insert/delete reduces to a width-$w$ content edit straddling the cut — the aligned-frame reduction (§5 measures it is real). Everything algebraic below lives in that aligned frame.

The one-line lesson, one level up. A structural edit is local only in coordinates attached to content (token-attached blocks). Index-attached blocking manufactures a fake "all-blocks-changed." Same relative-vs-absolute-PE lesson, one level up: local in every structure attached to content, global in every structure attached to coordinates.

2. Insert as bordering — the predictor, derived

Insert a token $\nu$ at cut $c$. In the aligned frame the new operator is the old $M$ bordered by $\nu$'s couplings:

$$ M' = \begin{pmatrix} M & b \\ c^{\top} & \delta \end{pmatrix}, \qquad b=(\text{old}\to\nu),\; c^{\top}=(\nu\to\text{old}),\; \delta=(\text{self}). $$

Where the blocks come from

They are not free parameters — they are Jacobian blocks of the cell, gotten from the same autodiff the DEQ already runs, restricted to the cut window:

$$ b=-\tfrac{\partial f_{\text{old}}}{\partial z_\nu},\qquad c^{\top}=-\tfrac{\partial f_\nu}{\partial z_{A}},\qquad \delta=I-\tfrac{\partial f_\nu}{\partial z_\nu}. $$

$\delta$ is one jacrev on $\nu$'s own update; $c^{\top}$ is $\nu$'s Jacobian row (a VJP); $b$ is a JVP seeded at the new slot. Windowed attention makes $b,c^{\top}$ nonzero only for old tokens within $W$ of the cut — cost $O(Wd^{2})$, cut-local, length-independent. Crucially, evaluated where: for the predictor you differentiate at the warm point (cached old states $z^\star_{A}$ in $\nu$'s window + the fresh slot), not at the true new fixed point $z_2^\star$. That is what makes it cheap — and it is exactly the frozen-operator approximation whose error §4 quantifies.

The linear system, and what each symbol is

Linearize the cell around the old fixed point: $f(z,h)\approx Jz+g$ with $g:=f(z^\star,h)-Jz^\star$ the effective injection, so $z^\star=Rg$, $R=M^{-1}$. The new (first-order) equilibrium solves

$$ \begin{pmatrix} M & b \\ c^{\top} & \delta \end{pmatrix}\! \begin{pmatrix} z_{A} \\ z_{\nu}\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} g \\ g_{\nu}\end{pmatrix}, $$
  • $z^\star_{A}=Rg$ — cached old-token states (known);
  • $z_{A}$ — old-token states in the new equilibrium (aligned indices);
  • $z_{\nu}$ — the inserted token's state ($d$-vector);
  • $g$ — old effective injection, the same in both systems away from the cut (the aligned-frame invariance — this is the substantive physical input, see below);
  • $g_{\nu}$ — the new token's effective injection, from its input $h_\nu$ (the only genuinely new forcing).

Eliminate the new slot → the source $u$

From the top row, $z_{A}=Rg-Rb\,z_{\nu}=z^\star_{A}-Rb\,z_{\nu}$. Substitute into the bottom row:

$$ \underbrace{(\delta-c^{\top}Rb)}_{S}\,z_{\nu}=g_{\nu}-c^{\top}Rg=g_{\nu}-c^{\top}z^\star_{A}. $$

So the predictor is two cut-local steps:

  1. New slot: $\;z_{\nu}=S^{-1}\big(g_{\nu}-c^{\top}z^\star_{A}\big)$ — $\nu$'s injection minus what the cached old equilibrium drives into it through its window ($c^{\top}z^\star_{A}$, a cut-local matvec), inverted by the new-token stiffness $S=\delta-c^{\top}Rb$ (needs $Rb$ = $R$ on $b$'s $d$ cut-local columns; one $d\times d$ solve — the only new inverse).
  2. Old-block move: $\;u=-\,b\,z_{\nu}$, then $\;\delta z_{A}=R\,u.$

That is how $u$ is formed: $u=-b\,z_\nu$, the new slot's state pushed back onto the old tokens through the coupling $b$. Because $b$ is cut-supported, $u$ is a cut-window vector in the old $N$-space, and $R_{\text{old}}u$ is the screened old-block displacement. Every object is cached ($R$, $z^\star_A$) or a $d\times d$ block ($S$, $\delta$).

Why "$z^\star=Rg$" earns its keep — it is bookkeeping, not a solve. Taken literally $z^\star=Rg$ is circular: $g$ is defined from $z^\star$, so you can't solve for $z^\star$ with it (the exception is a genuinely affine $f$, where it is exact). Its real job is a cancellation: the insert leaves the old tokens' injection $g$ unchanged away from the cut (relative-PE / aligned-frame invariance), so the same $g$ sits in both systems and drops out of the difference — $\delta z_{A}=z_{A}-z^\star_{A}=-Rb\,z_{\nu}$, no $g$ in sight. That cancellation is why the footprint is cut-local: the forcing changed only at the new slot and the cut band. We never use the value of $g$, only (i) that it is shared, and (ii) the cached state $z^\star_{A}$ it produces.

Theorem (a): the per-edit two-term envelope — spatial

Bounding each leg of $\delta z_A = Rb\,z_\nu$ with the cached tier-1 reach envelope (a resolvent column sourced at the cut decays at the old rate) gives the new response reach

$$ \big\|[R_{\text{new}}]_{ij}\big\| \;\le\; \underbrace{C\,\rho^{\,d(i,j)}}_{\text{direct}} \;+\; \underbrace{\tfrac{C'C''}{\sigma_{\min}(S)}\,\rho^{\,d(i,\text{cut})+d(\text{cut},j)}}_{\text{through }\nu}. $$

Physically $\nu$ is a scattering center at the cut: any $i\to j$ response gains one extra path (in to the cut, scatter with gain $1/\sigma_{\min}(S)$, out to $j$), each leg decaying at $\rho$. By the triangle inequality $d(i,\text{cut})+d(\text{cut},j)\ge d(i,j)$, the correction never decays slower than the direct term — a subdominant bump localized at the cut. Rate inherited, constant picks up one factor. The only new number is the scalar gate $\sigma_{\min}(S)$, from local blocks.

Theorem (b): the cumulative Stein budget — temporal

A different question: not "does this edit's envelope hold" but "how many edits before the cached geometry goes stale." With the Stein RHS normalized to $-I$ (margin $\eta=1$), each edit spends $\lambda_{\max}(\Delta)$, where $\Delta=\tilde G^{*}P\tilde E+\tilde E^{*}P\tilde G+\tilde E^{*}P\tilde E$ is Hermitian, rank $O(d)$, localized at the cut. The cached Gramian $P$ stays valid while $\eta-\sum\lambda_{\max}(\Delta)>0$; cross it and recertify. Keep (a) and (b) separate — (a) per-edit and spatial, (b) cumulative and temporal.

All tiers inherit. Tier-1 reach: the correction is a resolvent column sourced at the cut, decaying $\rho^{\,d(\text{cut})}$ (the measured rank 2–6 far field, §5). Tier-2 residual: holds on the new system, new conditioning carried solely by $\sigma_{\min}(S)$. Directional/Woodbury: rank-$d$ Schur update of $R$. Reader-set/DWR: $w_{\text{reader}}\!\cdot u$ is a single inner product — certifies whether the edit changes a given reader's output with no re-solve.

3. Delete — the dual, and it's even cheaper

Deletion removes token $\nu$ from a system where it was present. Reuse the same algebra in reverse. Pre-delete, the full equilibrium $(z^\star_{A},z^\star_{\nu})$ satisfied $Mz^\star_{A}+bz^\star_{\nu}=g$. Post-delete, the remaining tokens solve $Mz_{A}=g$ (same $g$ in the aligned frame; $\nu$'s coupling gone), so $z_{A}=Rg$. Subtract:

$$ \delta z_{A}^{\text{del}} \;=\; z_{A}-z^\star_{A} \;=\; +\,R\,b\,z^\star_{\nu}. $$

The mirror of insert ($-Rb\,z_\nu$) — but with a decisive simplification: the deleted token's state $z^\star_\nu$ is already in the cache (it was part of the old equilibrium), so there is no Schur solve. Deletion's predictor is literally $R_{\text{old}}$ (cached, banded) applied to the cut-local vector $b\,z^\star_\nu$ — cheaper than insert. On the resolvent side this is the standard downdating

$$ R^{\text{del}}_{AA}=R_{AA}-R_{A\nu}\,(R_{\nu\nu})^{-1}\,R_{\nu A}, $$

subtract every resolvent path routed through $\nu$; the conditioning gate is $\|R_{\nu\nu}^{-1}\|$ = the deleted token's diagonal resolvent block, i.e. its hub-ness (a well-connected token is expensive to remove). As with insert there is also a cut-local band update $\Delta M_{\text{band}}$: the old tokens that had $\nu$ in their window lose it, changing their update — computed by the same autodiff at the cut.

4. Keeping the band exact: local re-blocking at the cut

The bordering above certifies via banded body + rank-$d$ border. There is a complementary move that keeps the operator exactly banded, so the Note-#11 block-tridiagonal certificate applies verbatim with no low-rank term at all. The certificate partitions the sequence into width-$W$ windows so that $J$ is block-bidiagonal (causal) / tridiagonal (bidir) — each block couples only to its neighbours. A structural edit misaligns that partition: at $W=10$, a deletion leaves the cut window width 9, and every downstream window boundary shifts by one token, so a fixed-index partition is no longer clean.

The fix is to re-block locally, not globally. Merge the width-9 cut window with an adjacent width-10 window into one width-19 super-block. Inside it, the misalignment is absorbed; outside it, the boundaries snap back to the original grid (aligned frame — far windows are byte-identical under relative PE). The result is a valid block-bidiagonal / tridiagonal partition with a single enlarged diagonal block at the cut. Exact bandedness — and the reach/Stein certificate — is restored by paying one fat block ($O(Wd)$ larger), not a global re-partition.

This is the partition-level twin of the aligned-frame reduction: an insert/delete is a local re-blocking at the cut. Two equivalent readings of the same edit, pick per use: the rank-$d$ border (keep the old blocking, add a low-rank scattering term — best when you want to reuse the cached $R$ and gate on $\sigma_{\min}(S)$), or the width-19 re-block (keep it exactly banded, one larger block — best when you want the block-tridiagonal certificate to apply with no low-rank bookkeeping). The re-block localizes the churn to the merged block; everything beyond it is untouched.

5. The measured shadow: how far does an edit reach?

c2_insertdelete.py (n=8, gap-40). A null filler insert/delete in the filler region, diffed in the aligned frame, normalized by per-position state norm. The clean isolate is the positional-shadow ratio = insert far-field / substitution far-field on the same architecture, PE the only difference:

checkpointPEshadow ratioreading
bidir40bidir + ABS70×dense positional-reindex shadow (every shifted $h_0$ changed; downstream-dense 0.84)
bidirnp40bidir + REL2×insert ≈ substitution-at-cut — the aligned-frame reduction
curr40causal + ABS2×small but confounded (near-singular gap-40 → substitutions already reach far, ratio understates)

Relative PE cuts the insert shadow ~33× (70→2), isolated to the PE choice. Plus: far-field rank 2–6 of $d{=}64$ across all (structural-edit shadows compress to the carry "highway" → certifiable by a rank-$r$ update); causal up_rel $\approx 0$ (shadow strictly downstream); and warm < cold at gap-40 (curr 25/53, bidir 18/34, bidirnp 38/64 iters) — structural edits are maintainable by the aligned-frame warm start.

Caveats. gap-40 is near-singular so the far field is thin and magnitudes large — the ratio is the signal, not the magnitudes; $n=8$; gap-24 too short to have a far field; bidirnp24 warm>cold (68/44) is the known near-singular outlier. A clean causal-relative test and a gap $\ge 60$ substrate are owed.

6. The negative: no cheap rigorous bordering adjoint

The tempting deployment claim: reuse the pre-edit reader adjoint, correct it with a Schur/Woodbury term from cut-local blocks, and get a rigorous post-insert bound for free. colored_dwr_bordering.py tested it; it fails cleanly. Woodbury reconstruction is exact only when the support is the whole sequence:

support $S$ used$\Delta M$ mass capturedreconstruction
$\pm 8$ around cut~55% (45% outside)poor
$\pm 16$ around cut~83% (17% outside)partial
whole sequence100%$\sim 10^{-15}$ (exact)

Cause — state dependence. $J$ depends on $z$, and the insert shifts the equilibrium everywhere it reaches (rate $\rho(G)$), so $\Delta M=J(z_2^\star)-J(z^\star)$ is global, not window-local: exact bordering needs $S\approx L$, and no $O(Wd)$ rigorous cheap correction exists. This refutes an earlier overclaim: the §2 algebra is exact for the frozen-operator response, but the operator does not stay frozen across a re-solve.

What survives — a useful partial. A warm-local bordering (correct the adjoint over a local support) recovers flip discrimination on long-range edits that the naive cached adjoint misses (naive $0\times \to$ warm-Schur $13\text{–}20\times \approx$ oracle $13\text{–}29\times$), staying 100% sound — but it reconstructs the warm operator, not $R_2$ (recon error 20–990% on large edits), so it is a cheap approximate discriminator, not a rigorous bound. Net: cheap sound bound (naive) and cheap approximate long-range discrimination (warm-bordering); rigorous exact = re-solve.

7. Warm-start vs warmer-than-warm — precisely

Three inits for the post-edit re-solve, in increasing quality; the last two are the whole efficiency story.

initwhat you seedcost
Coldzeros on the new systemfull solve
Partial warm-startaligned copy: old $z^\star$ at shifted indices + fresh slot (insert) / drop slot (delete)free
Warmer-than-warmaligned copy plus the predicted move: old blocks $\leftarrow z^\star_A+\delta z_A$, new slot $\leftarrow z_\nu$one $R$-action on cut-local $u$ + a $d\times d$ solve
  • Partial warm-start is the structural analog of value-edit warm-start (reuse old $z^\star$). It is partial because the sizes differ — you can reuse only the aligned positions, and must supply the new slot (insert) or drop it (delete). Its leverage: only the cut's $\xi$-ball actually re-settles; the far field is already converged (that is the §5 shadow decay), so the solver moves a local neighbourhood, not the sequence. This is what "warm < cold" measures.
  • Warmer-than-warm improves that seed by the §2/§3 predictor: add $\delta z_A=R\,u$ ($u=-b\,z_\nu$ for insert, $u=+b\,z^\star_\nu$ for delete) to the aligned old states, and use $z_\nu$ for the new slot instead of a naive init. It seeds the re-solve inside the cut ball closer to the answer, trimming iterations. Delete is cheapest — $z^\star_\nu$ is cached, so no Schur solve.
When warmer-than-warm actually pays. The predictor is one resolvent action on a cut-local $u$ $\approx$ one local iteration's work, so it pays iff it saves more than one Broyden iteration (toy: 15–30% = 2–4 iters) and $R$ is amortized across many edits to a fixed base. Recompute-$R$-per-edit is a wash. The amortized regime is RAG (insert/delete into a fixed cached base), which flips wash → net win: append is free ($b=0$, prefix preserved), a mid-insert is one-sided on the causal face, and the query is a known reader so DWR meters "does this chunk change the answer" ($w_{\text{query}}\!\cdot u$) with no re-solve for inert chunks.
It is an initializer, not a certificate. Warmer-than-warm is the frozen-operator, first-order prediction — it uses the cached $R_{\text{old}}$, never $R_{\text{new}}$. The displacement $\delta z$ it predicts is screened (§5), but the operator update $R_{\text{new}}-R_{\text{old}}$ is diffuse (§6), which is exactly why there is no one-shot exact update — the diffuse remainder is what the re-solve cleans up. Both inits converge to the same fixed point; the predictor only changes how fast.

8. Placement

Three claim-strengths, never mixed: the bordering algebra, the two theorems, the delete dual, and the re-blocking are theory (exact for the frozen operator); the shadow ratio (70→2×), the rank 2–6 far field, and warm<cold are measured; the cheap rigorous adjoint is a measured negative (state dependence delocalizes $\Delta M$); the warm-vs-warmer net-win and RAG metering are proposed, pending C7. Headline: an insert/delete is a scattering center at the cut whose response reach is cheap and cut-local, but whose operator update across a re-solve is not — so reuse the geometry (rank-$d$ border or a width-19 re-block), price the recert, and re-solve for the exact answer.
