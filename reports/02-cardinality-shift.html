Cardinality Shift: Do the Equilibrium Properties Survive "Train Small, Infer Large"?

Zhibai Jia · 19 June 2026 · working notes, not for circulation beyond reviewers

1. Where we left off

Note #1 established, on a synthetic cluster-counting task at a fixed set size (N=24), a clean dissociation: a contractive update (spectral norm or LayerNorm on a mean-pool aggregator) yields an exactly unique fixed point — and therefore exact path independence and exact unlearning — while the more expressive attention aggregator buys accuracy at the cost of genuine non-uniqueness. This note asks the next question: what happens when we change the set size at inference?

This is the "train small, infer large" promise that makes equilibrium models attractive — train once on small sets, then spend more inference compute to handle larger ones. We train each model once at N=24 and probe, without retraining, at N = 24, 48, 96, 192 (1× to 8×). The number of clusters stays k ∈ {1..4}; only the density grows, so this isolates cardinality from task difficulty.

4. The leakage tail (attention)

The most important pattern is the gap between the mean and the worst-case unlearning gap for attention. On average, removing a point is nearly exact — but the worst case stays large at every cardinality. That persistent tail is where an adversary trying to recover a removed point would operate.

5. Predictions vs. outcome

PropertyPredictedObserved
Well-posednesssurvives Confirmed — mean-pool conv ~1.0 at all N; attention conv 0.89–0.96, flat
Path independencedegrades with N Not confirmed — it is an architecture property, not a scale effect: mean-pool exact at every N, attention non-unique at every N
Unlearningmean shrinks, tail persists Tail confirmed (max ~0.5–0.75 at all N); mean does not monotonically shrink
Efficiencyadvantage widens Confirmed — cold iters grow with N (84→112, 92→120); warm grows slower
Headline. Train-small / infer-large preserves the equilibrium properties. Convergence survives 8× scaling; the contractive models stay exactly unique and unlearnable at every size; and attention's accuracy even improves with N (0.78 → 0.835) — a clean cardinality-generalization result. The predicted "crack at scale" did not appear: uniqueness is determined by the architecture (contractive vs. expressive), not by set size.

6. A methodological note worth recording

These numbers come from TorchDEQ's implicit/phantom gradient. An earlier pass that trained by unrolling a truncated solver produced qualitatively different (and misleading) conclusions — it reported spectral normalization "diverging" and the properties as only approximate. The reason: spectral norm caps each linear layer at Lipschitz 1, but because the latent enters the update twice (directly and via the pool), the composite map's Lipschitz bound is ~√2 — so contraction is permitted, not guaranteed. Unrolled training optimizes a truncated transient with no incentive to reach a true fixed point, and settles in the expansive regime; the implicit gradient optimizes the actual fixed point and is pushed into the contractive one. The lesson: for equilibrium models, the training signal, not just the architecture, decides whether a real fixed point exists.

7. Next steps

  • Adversarial phase (Layer 2): a membership-inference attack targeting attention's persistent worst-case tail — does the leakage shown here actually let an attacker recover a removed point? Then differential-privacy noise masking of the residual.
  • Jacobian characterization (Layer 3): measure the spectral radius of the update at the fixed point to locate where attention's uniqueness fails (the bifurcation set) — which also confirms the unrolled-vs-implicit story above (expansive vs. contractive realized maps).
